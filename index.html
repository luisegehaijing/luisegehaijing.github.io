I am a Computer Science PhD student at Washington University in St Louis, advised by Yevgeniy Vorobeychik and Brendan Juba. I spend most of my time over the theoretical aspects of computing, ranging from learning theory to social choice and game theory.

On a personal note, I would like to describe myself as a generalist as my interests are quite broad. I started out in the cognitive science major at the University of Edinburgh, with the passion to figure out how mind works. While this curiosity has never diminished, it just happened that the beauty of maths struck me greatly. I ended up getting a master degree specialised in geometry and topology at Imperial College London under the guidance of the amazing Paolo Cascini. My master thesis is on algebraic geometry inspired by the wonder idea called ``the periodic table of shapes" .

I have turned back to CS because of the philosophical implications and social impact of many compuational problems. Note that I have been trying to avoid the locution "AI" because its interpretations have been overloaded.

  • Learning utilities from pairwise comparisons
    Luise Ge, Brendan Juba and Yevegniy Vorobeychik
  • Fair allocation: implementing and evaluating an algorithm for competitive allocation of chores Outstanding undergraduate thesis at University of Edinburgh
    Our project targets a specific kind of allocation problems, namely the allocation of divisible bads, with additive disutilities and no money transfer. The competitive rule dominates the world of divisible goods allocation because of the celebrated Eisenberg-Gale’s convex programming algorithm. Their result demonstrates that competitive allocations can be computed easily (as in polynomial time) by maximising Nash social welfare. However, computing competitive allocation for chores is difficult as the solutions can be wildly multi-valued and discontinuous, corresponding to all critical points of Nash Social Welfare on the Pareto frontier.
